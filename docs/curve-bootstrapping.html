Curve Bootstrapping

Your interest rate swap prices at 102 bps. Your model says 98 bps. The gap almost always lives in how the discount or forward curve was built — specifically, which market instruments anchored each pillar.

Morning desk scenario: You receive a €100M notional EURIBOR 3M swap maturing in 5 years. Before you can hedge it or book a risk number, you need two separate curves: an OIS discount curve (to PV every cash flow) and a EURIBOR 3M forward curve (to project what the floating rate will fix at on each reset date). Both are built by bootstrapping — stripping a discount factor from each market quote in turn, shortest maturity first. This page shows you exactly which instruments go in, what discount factors and zero rates come out, and why the two curves are necessarily different.

Discount Curves vs Forward Curves

A swap has two legs. You need to know what each future cash flow is worth today (discount curve) and, for the floating leg, what rate it will fix at on each reset date (forward curve). These are different questions answered by different curves.

Why did two curves replace one? Before 2008, a single LIBOR curve handled both discounting and forward projection. The financial crisis exposed LIBOR as embedding significant bank credit and liquidity risk — it was too high to use as a risk-free discount rate. Since then, OIS rates (anchored to central bank overnight rates, nearly risk-free) discount all collateralised derivatives. IBOR curves survive only for projecting the floating rates of instruments that reference them. Two curves, two jobs.

Discount Curve OIS / Risk-Free

Question it answers: what is €1 received in 5 years worth today?

  • Built from OIS swap rates (ESTR in EUR, SOFR in USD, SONIA in GBP)
  • Short end anchored by the overnight deposit rate (= central bank rate)
  • Output: discount factors P(0,T) at each pillar tenor
  • Used to PV all cash flows regardless of product type or floating index
  • CSA / collateral agreements reference OIS, so counterparties expect OIS discounting
  • No tenor basis — one OIS curve per currency

Forward Curve EURIBOR 3M

Question it answers: what will 3M EURIBOR fix at in 2 years?

  • Built from 3M deposits, FRAs, IMM futures, and EURIBOR IRS
  • Encodes the market’s expectation of future EURIBOR fixings
  • Output: forward rates F(T₁,T₂) — the implied 3M fixing at each future date
  • Used only to project floating cash flows of EURIBOR-indexed products
  • Cash flows from this curve are then discounted with the OIS curve
  • Separate forward curve exists for each IBOR tenor (1M, 3M, 6M, 12M)
Bootstrapping in one sentence: For each pillar tenor T, find the discount factor P(0,T) such that the market instrument with that maturity re-prices to par (NPV = 0) — using all previously solved discount factors for shorter tenors as known inputs.
OIS vs non-OIS spread = term bank credit risk. The gap between the OIS zero rate and the EURIBOR 3M zero rate at the same tenor is not noise — it reflects the credit and liquidity risk of unsecured interbank lending over that period. In normal markets this spread is 15–30 bp. In stress (e.g. March 2020, March 2023) it widens to 60–100+ bp. If your system uses one curve for both, it is mispricing every EURIBOR-indexed product.

OIS Discount Curve ESTR — EUR

The OIS curve runs from overnight to 30 years. The very short end is anchored by the actual central bank deposit rate; the belly and long end are anchored by OIS swap rates quoted in the interbank market.

How an OIS swap constrains a discount factor: An OIS swap pays a fixed rate R against the daily-compounded ESTR. The floating leg exactly replicates a zero-coupon bond — so its NPV is simply 1 − P(0,T). Setting fixed = floating and solving for the unknown pillar discount factor gives: P(0,Tₙ) = [1 − R · ∑ P(0,Tᵢ) · τᵢ] / [1 + R · τₙ]. Each new pillar uses all previously solved DFs, working from short to long.

Bootstrap derivation — OIS

Step-by-step derivation for each pillar:

Short end (O/N Deposit, simple interest, ACT/360):
  P(0,T) = 1 / (1 + R · T)

Sub-annual OIS swap (single period, <1Y):
  P(0,T) = 1 / (1 + R · T)

Annual OIS swap (multi-period, annual coupon dates T₁...Tₙ):
  Fixed leg: R · ∑ᵢ₋₁ⁿ P(0,Tᵢ) · τᵢ
  Float leg: 1 − P(0,Tₙ)    [compounded ON = ZCB]

  NPV = 0  =>  P(0,Tₙ) = [1 − R · ∑ᵢ₋₁ⁿˉ¹ P(0,Tᵢ)] / [1 + R · τₙ]

Zero rate from DF:
  r(T) = −ln P(0,T) / T          (continuously compounded)

Forward DF between T₁ and T₂:
  P(T₁,T₂) = P(0,T₂) / P(0,T₁)

Forward rate:
  f(T₁,T₂) = [P(0,T₁)/P(0,T₂) − 1] / (T₂−T₁)

EURIBOR 3M Forward Curve Projection

This curve is built to answer one question: what 3M EURIBOR rate will the market project at each future reset date? Deposits anchor the near end, FRAs cover the 3M–2Y belly, and par IRS rates extend the curve to 30 years — all discounted using the OIS curve.

Dual-curve bootstrapping: When you bootstrap a 5Y EURIBOR swap, the fixed leg PV uses OIS discount factors. You are solving for the EURIBOR projection curve’s own discount factor at 5Y — a completely separate object from the OIS DF — that makes the swap price to par. The OIS curve is an external input to this bootstrap; the output is the projection curve DFs, from which 3M forward rates are derived.

Bootstrap derivation — EURIBOR dual-curve

Three instrument types, three formulas:

3M Deposit (direct, simple interest):
  Pᵒ(0, 3M) = 1 / (1 + R · 0.25)

FRA for period [T₁, T₂]:
  R = F(T₁,T₂) = [Pᵒ(0,T₁)/Pᵒ(0,T₂) − 1] / τ
  => Pᵒ(0,T₂) = Pᵒ(0,T₁) / (1 + R · τ)

Par IRS vs EURIBOR 3M (annual fixed, quarterly float):
  Fixed leg:  PV_fix = R · ∑ P_OIS(0,Tᵢ) · τᵢ       [OIS DFs]
  Float leg:  PV_flt = ∑ F_j · τ_j · P_OIS(0,T_j)  [projected fwds, OIS discount]
  where F_j = [Pᵒ(0,T_{j-1})/Pᵒ(0,T_j) − 1] / τ_j

  NPV = 0, all F_j known except last => solve for Pᵒ(0,T_last):
  PV_fix − PV_flt_known = [Pᵒ(0,T_{n-1})/Pᵒ(0,T_n) − 1] · τ · P_OIS(0,T_n)
  => Pᵒ(0,T_n) = Pᵒ(0,T_{n-1}) / [1 + (PV_fix − PV_flt_known)/(τ · P_OIS(0,T_n))]

Glossary

Discount FactorP(0,T): the present value of €1 received at time T. Always between 0 and 1; declines monotonically with tenor. Zero RateContinuously-compounded rate r(T) such that e^(−r·T) = P(0,T). Represents the pure time-value of money at tenor T with no coupon reinvestment assumption. Par Swap RateThe fixed rate that makes a vanilla swap NPV = 0 at inception. Bootstrapping strips zero rates from these par rates one tenor at a time. OISOvernight Index Swap. Exchanges a fixed rate vs daily-compounded overnight rate (ESTR/SOFR/SONIA). Nearly risk-free; used as the universal discount curve post-2008. ESTREuro Short-Term Rate. The ECB’s overnight unsecured benchmark, volume-weighted across eligible transactions. Replaced EONIA in 2022. Anchors the EUR OIS curve. EURIBOR 3MEuro Interbank Offered Rate, 3-month tenor. Panel-based unsecured lending rate. Typically 15–35 bp above OIS; the spread widens in credit stress. FRAForward Rate Agreement. Locks in a rate for a future period, e.g. “3×6” = 3M EURIBOR starting in 3 months. Primary mid-curve inputs for the EURIBOR forward curve. Forward RateImplied rate between two future dates: F(T₁,T₂) = [P(0,T₁)/P(0,T₂)−1]/τ. Extracted from bootstrapped DFs; used to project IBOR fixings. PillarA tenor at which a discount factor is directly solved from one market instrument. Between pillars, DFs are obtained by interpolation (usually log-linear in DF = linear in zero rate). Dual-CurvePost-2008 framework: cash flows are projected using an IBOR curve but discounted using the OIS curve. Also called multi-curve. Single-curve pricing misprices every collateralised IBOR swap. Log-Linear Interp.Standard interpolation between pillars: ln P(T) is linear in T, equivalent to linear interpolation of zero rates. Ensures discount factors are always positive and forward rates are smooth. BootstrappingIterative procedure that solves for one unknown discount factor per market instrument, starting from the shortest maturity and using all previously solved DFs as inputs.
