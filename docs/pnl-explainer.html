Every trading desk runs a daily PnL explain. The goal: if risk systems said you had $500k DV01, and rates moved 10bp, you should have made roughly $5M. Did you? If actual PnL is $6.2M, the extra $1.2M came from somewhere — gamma, vol moves, or unexplained model residual.

Small unexplained PnL = model is working well. Large unexplained = higher-order risks not captured, or a model/data issue. Under FRTB, regulators require the unexplained to stay below a threshold as a condition for using an internal model.

For linear instruments (swaps, bonds): PnL is almost entirely DV01 × ΔR. Gamma is negligible. Vega is zero.
For non-linear instruments (swaptions, caps, floors): gamma, vega, volga, and vanna all contribute meaningfully — especially in large or vol-rich moves.

The Taylor Expansion

The core idea: instead of repricing a position from scratch every time markets move, we approximate the price change using pre-computed sensitivities — the Greeks. Each Greek is multiplied by the relevant market move to give its dollar contribution.

The idea in one sentence: if you know how sensitive a position is to each market input, and you know how much each input moved, you can add up the contributions to estimate the total PnL — without rerunning a slow pricing model.

This is called a first- and second-order Taylor expansion of the pricing function. The first-order terms (DV01, Vega) are linear estimates. The second-order terms (Gamma, Volga, Vanna) correct for curvature — the fact that sensitivities themselves change as markets move. What remains after summing all terms is the unexplained — the third-order and higher terms the expansion truncates.

DV01 × ΔR  · first order
The bread-and-butter of rates PnL. DV01 is your dollar gain per basis-point move in rates. Multiply by how far rates moved. For swaps and bonds, this is the entire story.
½ Γ (ΔR)²  · second order
Gamma corrects for the fact that your DV01 changes as rates move. A large move in either direction benefits a long-gamma position — the PnL curve bends upward. Proportional to the square of the move.
ν × Δσ  · first order
Vega is your sensitivity to a 1bp change in implied vol. When vol rises (market becomes more uncertain), long-option positions gain. This term only applies to non-linear instruments — a plain swap has zero vega.
½ 𝒱 (Δσ)²  · second order
Volga (vol gamma) corrects the linear vega estimate for large vol moves. OTM swaptions have high Volga — when vol jumps 30bp, vega alone understates the gain significantly.
𝒜 × ΔR × Δσ  · cross term
Vanna captures the interaction between a rate move and a vol move. It shows up whenever rates and vol move together — common in crises. Neither the DV01 term nor the Vega term alone captures it.
Θ × Δt  · time decay
Theta is the daily bleed — the cost of holding an option as it ages. Long options always lose theta. It is the price you pay for the convexity benefit of being long gamma and long vega.
\[\Delta P \;\approx\; \underbrace{\sum_i \text{DV01}_i \cdot \Delta R_i}_{\text{DV01 PnL}} \;+\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\Gamma_{ij}\cdot\Delta R_i\Delta R_j}_{\text{Gamma PnL (Hessian)}} \;+\; \underbrace{\nu\cdot\Delta\sigma}_{\text{Vega PnL}} \;+\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathcal{V}\cdot(\Delta\sigma)^2}_{\text{Volga PnL}} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathcal{A}\cdot\Delta R\cdot\Delta\sigma}_{\text{Vanna PnL}} \;+\; \underbrace{\Theta\cdot\Delta t}_{\text{Theta PnL}}\]

Position Setup

This section defines the position whose PnL we will explain. Every chart on this page updates to reflect the position you set here.

What is a swaption? A swaption is an option to enter an interest rate swap. A payer swaption gives you the right to pay fixed and receive floating — it profits when rates rise. A receiver swaption is the opposite. They are the primary non-linear instrument on a rates desk.

The key parameters are: the option expiry (how long until you must decide whether to enter the swap), the swap tenor (how long the resulting swap would run), and the normal vol (the market's implied uncertainty about where rates will be at expiry).

In Derived mode, all six Greeks are computed automatically from the Bachelier model. In Manual mode, you set the Greeks yourself — useful if you already know your book's risk numbers and just want to explore how they translate to PnL.

For rates that can go negative, the Normal (Bachelier) model treats rates as an arithmetic Brownian motion. A payer swaption price is:

\[V = A \cdot \left[(F-K)\,\mathcal{N}(d) + \sigma_N\sqrt{T}\,\mathcal{n}(d)\right], \quad d = \frac{F-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T}}\]

where \(A\) is the swap annuity, \(F\) the forward swap rate, \(K\) the strike, \(\sigma_N\) the normal vol, \(\mathcal{N}\) and \(\mathcal{n}\) the normal CDF and PDF. The model is standard for EUR/GBP rates desks and any environment where rates trade near zero or negative.

The Hessian — Curve Cross-Gamma

In equity options, gamma is a single number. In rates, the curve has many points — 1Y, 2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y — and each pair of points has its own gamma. The Hessian matrix collects all of them.

Why does this matter? Consider a 2y10y swaption — an option expiring in 2 years on a 10-year swap. Its gamma is spread across the 2Y and 10Y parts of the curve. If both move together (a parallel shift), you get cross-gamma PnL from the interaction. If they move in opposite directions (a flattener), the cross-gamma can partially cancel the diagonal gamma.

Diagonal cells (Γᵢᵢ): own-gamma at a single tenor — the curvature of your PnL if only that one tenor moves. This is the number you'd see in a simple equity options world.

Off-diagonal cells (Γᵢⱼ, i≠j): cross-gamma between tenor i and tenor j. Non-zero whenever a position spans more than one tenor. A book with many different swaption expiries and swap tenors will have a rich, dense Hessian. This is why rates PnL explain requires a matrix, not just a single gamma.

\[H = \begin{pmatrix} \Gamma_{1Y,1Y} & \Gamma_{1Y,2Y} & \cdots & \Gamma_{1Y,30Y} \\ \Gamma_{2Y,1Y} & \Gamma_{2Y,2Y} & \cdots & \Gamma_{2Y,30Y} \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\ \Gamma_{30Y,1Y} & \cdots & \cdots & \Gamma_{30Y,30Y} \end{pmatrix} \qquad \text{Gamma PnL} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta\mathbf{R}^\top H\,\Delta\mathbf{R}\]

Diagonal (own-gamma): Γᵢᵢ tells you how the DV01 at tenor i changes when rate i moves. A 10Y swaption has large Γ₁₀ᵧ,₁₀ᵧ.

Off-diagonal (cross-gamma): Γᵢⱼ (i≠j) tells you how the DV01 at tenor i changes when rate j moves. A 2y10y swaption has significant Γ₂ᵧ,₁₀ᵧ — a parallel shift hits both legs.

A flattener (short rates up, long rates down) can have zero or negative gamma PnL even with long-gamma positions, because the cross-gamma terms cancel the diagonal terms. This is why a Hessian — not a single gamma number — is needed for a rate book.

Second-Order Greeks

The first-order Greeks (DV01, Vega) tell you your sensitivity today. The second-order Greeks tell you how those sensitivities change as markets move. They are what make options different from linear instruments.

The intuition: imagine delta-hedging a swaption with a swap. Each day, you adjust the swap hedge so your net DV01 is zero. But rates keep moving, so the swaption's delta keeps changing — that daily re-hedging generates PnL. The faster the delta changes, the more PnL you earn from re-hedging. Gamma is the speed of that change with respect to rates, Volga with respect to vol, and Vanna captures the cross-effect between rates and vol together.

Select a tab below to explore each one. The charts update automatically as you change the position in the Position Setup section above.

What Gamma tells you: your DV01 is not fixed. As rates move, your rate sensitivity changes. Gamma measures how fast. A position with DV01 = $5,000 and Gamma = $50/bp² will have a DV01 of $5,500 after a 10bp move up — and $4,500 after a 10bp move down.

The PnL from this shifting DV01 is always positive for long-gamma positions — rates moving up increases the DV01 of a payer swaption (so you hold more of a winner), and rates moving down reduces it (so you hold less of a loser). This is the convexity benefit, and the chart on the right shows it as a parabola opening upward.

Short-gamma positions (sold swaptions) have the opposite: the DV01 grows against you on big moves. This is why short-gamma positions bleed on volatility, regardless of direction. The cost of holding long gamma is Theta — the daily time decay you pay to own that convexity.

\[\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial F^2} = A\,\frac{\mathcal{n}(d)}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T}}\]
Gamma PnL = ½ Γ (ΔR)² — always positive for long options regardless of rate direction. Gamma is highest ATM and near expiry.

What Volga tells you: Vega is your option's sensitivity to vol — the price you earn (or lose) per 1bp change in implied vol. But Vega itself is not constant. As vol rises, the Vega of an OTM swaption increases — it becomes more sensitive to further vol moves. Volga measures how fast that happens.

Think of it this way: if vol spikes from 80bp to 120bp (+40bp), linear vega says your gain = Vega × 40. But Volga says: after the first 20bp, your Vega has grown, so the second 20bp earns more. The true gain is larger than the linear estimate. The chart on the right shows this — the purple curve bends above the grey linear line.

ATM options have near-zero Volga (their Vega barely changes with vol). OTM options have high Volga — they are highly convex in vol space. This is why OTM swaptions are disproportionately valuable when vol explodes in a crisis, even if rates haven't moved much yet.

\[\mathcal{V} = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial\sigma^2} = A\,\sqrt{T}\,\mathcal{n}(d)\,\frac{d^2}{\sigma_N}\]
Volga PnL = ½ 𝒱 (Δσ)² — the curvature of option price in vol space. Zero ATM, grows as moneyness increases.

What Vanna tells you: Vanna is a cross-derivative — it captures the interaction between a rate move and a vol move. It answers: "if rates move AND vol moves at the same time, is there extra PnL that neither the DV01 term nor the Vega term accounts for?" The answer is yes, and Vanna is that extra piece.

Practically, Vanna tells you how your DV01 hedge ratio changes as vol moves. If you delta-hedge a swaption today (so net DV01 = 0) and overnight vol rises 20bp, your swaption's delta has shifted — you are no longer fully hedged. Vanna tells you by how much. This matters enormously in fast-moving markets where rate and vol moves are simultaneous.

The chart on the right shows DV01 as a function of the forward rate, plotted at three vol levels. The gap between the orange line (higher vol) and the blue line (lower vol) is the Vanna effect — the same rate position has a different DV01 depending on where vol is.

\[\mathcal{A} = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial F\,\partial\sigma} = -A\,\frac{\mathcal{n}(d)\,d}{\sigma_N}\]
Vanna = ∂(DV01)/∂σ = ∂(Vega)/∂F — the cross-sensitivity that appears when rates and vol move together.

Linear vs Non-linear — same DV01, different instrument

A classic question on a rates desk: "I can get the same DV01 with a swap or with a swaption. Why does it matter which I use?"

The answer is gamma. The two cards below show what happens to each instrument under the same net rate move from the attribution sliders above. The swap's PnL is a straight line — DV01 × ΔR, nothing else. The swaption earns the same DV01 PnL plus a gamma bonus that grows with the square of the move. In calm markets this bonus is small. Glossary

Hover any term for a definition.

DV01
Dollar Value of 01 (basis point). The PnL impact of a 1bp parallel shift in the rate curve. The primary first-order risk measure for rate instruments.
Gamma
Second derivative of price with respect to the underlying rate. For options, always positive if long (convexity benefit). Negative if short. $/bp² — how DV01 changes per bp of rate move.
Vega
Sensitivity of option price to a change in implied vol. For normal vol, Vega = A·√T·n(d). Measured in $/bp of normal vol change.
Volga
Also called Vomma. Second derivative of price with respect to vol — the "gamma of vega." OTM swaptions have large Volga: vol spikes amplify their value super-linearly.
Vanna
Mixed partial ∂²P/∂F∂σ. Tells you simultaneously how delta changes with vol and how vega changes with rates. Key in crisis scenarios where rates and vol move together.
Theta
Time decay — the daily erosion of option premium. Long options pay theta (negative). Short options collect it. Theta and gamma always work in opposite directions for the same position.
Hessian
The matrix of second partial derivatives. In rates, the 5×5 matrix of cross-gammas between tenor nodes. Required for accurate PnL explain on curve positions like steepeners or fly positions.
Normal Vol
Implied vol from the Bachelier (Normal) model — quoted in basis points. Unlike log-normal vol, it applies even when rates are near zero or negative. Standard for EUR and GBP rate markets.
Payer Swaption
Option to enter a swap paying fixed / receiving floating. Profits when rates rise. The rates equivalent of a call option on rates (or a put on bond prices).
Unexplained PnL
Actual PnL minus predicted (Taylor-expanded) PnL. In FRTB, this must stay below a regulatory threshold to use an internal model. Large unexplained indicates higher-order risk or model error.
Convexity
The benefit of positive gamma: large moves always help a long-gamma position. The option makes more on a 100bp move than twice what it makes on a 50bp move. This benefit is paid via theta.
Cross-Gamma
Γᵢⱼ = ∂²P/∂Rᵢ∂Rⱼ for i≠j. Non-zero whenever an instrument spans two tenor buckets. A 2y10y swaption has cross-gamma between the 2Y and 10Y nodes of the curve.
