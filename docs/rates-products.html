Linear

Interest Rate Swap

Exchange a fixed rate for a floating rate — or vice versa — without exchanging principal.

An IRS is the workhorse of the rates market. One party pays a fixed rate; the other pays a floating rate (historically LIBOR, now SOFR). Only the net difference changes hands on each payment date. No principal is exchanged.

Why it exists

A company that issues fixed-rate bonds but has floating-rate assets wants to convert its fixed liability into a floating one. A bank that lends at fixed rates but borrows in the overnight market wants to lock in a fixed income. The IRS separates the rate structure from the balance sheet — both sides get the exposure they want without refinancing.

On each payment date only the net is exchanged. If SOFR averaged 3.20%, fixed payer sends $150k (0.30% × $100M × 0.5). If SOFR averaged 3.80%, fixed receiver sends $150k. No principal moves.

Use cases

  • Corporate treasurer converting fixed bond liability to floating
  • Bank hedging fixed-rate loan book against rising SOFR
  • Rates trader taking a directional view on rate level
  • Asset manager matching liability duration

Common misconception

Both legs don't pay at the same time. The fixed leg typically pays semi-annually on 30/360. The floating (SOFR) leg pays quarterly or semi-annually in arrears, compounded daily during the period. On payment dates you net the two, but the accrual calendars may differ — off-the-run swaps can have mismatched payment dates that create unexpected cash flows.

Linear

OIS / SOFR Swap

A swap where the floating leg is the daily compounded overnight rate — now the standard for collateralised derivatives.

An Overnight Index Swap (OIS) is technically a specific type of IRS where the floating rate is an overnight rate compounded daily over each payment period. In the US this means SOFR; in the UK, SONIA; in the Eurozone, €STR. Post-LIBOR, most vanilla swaps are now OIS swaps.

Why OIS became the standard

LIBOR was a forward-looking rate (set at the start of each period) with a built-in bank credit risk premium — it reflected what banks would charge each other for 3-month unsecured lending. SOFR is backward-looking (the average is calculated after the period) and nearly credit-risk-free (it's based on overnight Treasury repo). Post-2023, SOFR is the standard floating reference for USD rates. The shift matters for pricing, discounting, and risk because SOFR ≠ LIBOR by the LIBOR-OIS spread (typically 10–30bp in normal markets).

How SOFR compounding works on the floating leg
Period: Jan 1 – Mar 31 (91 days). SOFR is observed each business day.
Compounded SOFR = [∏(1 + SOFR_i × d_i/360) − 1] × 360/91
where d_i = 1 (weekday) or 3 (Friday). Result is annualised and paid on Mar 31.

OIS vs IBOR swap — key differences

  • Backward-looking: SOFR rate isn't known until the period ends (5-day lookback common)
  • No term premium: SOFR has no bank credit risk, so OIS ≈ risk-free rate
  • Discounting: Collateralised derivatives are discounted at OIS — this is now the default
  • Convexity: SOFR-in-arrears vs SOFR-in-advance creates a small convexity difference

Common OIS tenors

1W, 1MMonetary policy and short-term liquidity

The publication lag trap

SOFR is published the next business day. For a swap paying on date T, the last few days of SOFR are unknown at settlement. The market convention uses a "5-day lookback" — the SOFR from 5 days ago is used for the last 5 days of the period. This means you need to understand which lookback convention your swap uses before you can settle it accurately.

Linear

Forward Rate Agreement (FRA)

Lock in a single interest rate for a single future period — the simplest building block of the yield curve.

A FRA is a single-period agreement to exchange a fixed rate for the floating rate over a defined future period. Unlike an IRS which has many cash flows, a FRA has exactly one. It is essentially a one-period swap settled at the start of the reference period (with a present-value discount).

Settlement is paid at the start of the reference period (not the end), so the cash flow is discounted at the actual floating rate: settlement = (SOFR − FRA_rate) × notional × days/360 / (1 + SOFR × days/360).

Notation: "3×6 FRA"

Read as "3-by-6." Starts in 3 months, ends 6 months from today. The reference period is the 3 months between month 3 and month 6. A "6×12 FRA" starts in 6 months and ends in 12 months.

FRA vs SOFR futures

  • FRA is OTC (bilateral); futures are exchange-traded
  • Futures have daily margin (creates convexity bias)
  • FRA rate = futures rate + convexity adjustment
  • FRAs are increasingly replaced by OIS swaps

Linear

SOFR Futures

Exchange-traded contract on the compounded SOFR rate — liquid, transparent, but with a systematic convexity bias vs OIS forwards.

CME 3-Month SOFR futures are priced as 100 minus the annualised compounded SOFR rate for the reference quarter. A price of 95.25 implies a rate of 4.75%. They are the most liquid short-rate instrument in the world and the primary tool for expressing near-term Fed policy views.

Why futures trade at a discount to OIS (the convexity adjustment)

Futures are marked to market daily. When rates rise, the short futures position gains immediately and can reinvest margin at the higher rate. When rates fall, the long position loses and must fund margin at the lower rate. This daily settlement asymmetry benefits short futures / hurts long futures — so a rational buyer of futures accepts a slightly lower rate (pays a slightly higher price). The futures rate is therefore systematically below the equivalent OIS forward rate. The gap = ½ × σ² × T₁ × T₂. At 100bp vol, a 2y contract is ~0.3bp cheap; a 5y contract is ~1.3bp cheap. See the Convexity page for the full strip calculation.

The DV01 mismatch with swaps

$25/bp per contract sounds small but adds up fast. To match a $10M 2y OIS swap (DV01 ~$2,000/bp), you need 80 contracts. The liquidity is deep enough near the front but thins out beyond the 2y point — at 5y and beyond, OIS swaps are typically more liquid than the equivalent futures strip.

Linear

Basis Swap

Exchange one floating rate for another — capturing the spread between two market segments that should, in theory, be the same.

A basis swap exchanges two floating-rate streams — no fixed leg. The most common USD basis swaps are SOFR vs Fed Funds (the FF/SOFR basis), 1M SOFR vs 3M SOFR, and in cross-currency, USD SOFR vs EUR €STR. One side typically pays flat; the other pays with a spread (the "basis").

Example: 5y SOFR vs 3M SOFR basis swap
Bank A
Daily compounded SOFR flat
← 3M SOFR term + 5bp spread
Bank B

The "basis" (5bp here) is the market price of the spread between these two rates. It fluctuates with funding conditions, credit perceptions, and supply/demand in each market.

Why basis ≠ zero

In theory, compounded daily SOFR over 3 months equals the 3M SOFR term rate. In practice they differ because:

  • Repo supply/demand fluctuates daily (SOFR is repo-based)
  • Quarter-end and year-end have large rate spikes
  • 3M SOFR term has a forward-looking premium
  • Different counterparties access each market at different costs

Common basis swaps

FF/SOFRFed Funds vs SOFR; tightens on stress
1M/3M SOFRTenor basis; reflects term premium
USD/EUR xccySOFR vs €STR + spread; FX-implied
SOFR/T-BillRepo vs government bill; credit stress
Basis swaps look boring until they're not

The FF/SOFR basis trades in a tight 5bp range for months, then blows out 30bp in a liquidity event. Banks that had large basis positions thought they were nearly flat — until they weren't. Basis is carry that tends to look good until a credit or funding event reprices everything. In March 2020, some basis swaps moved 50bp in a week.

Curve Product

CMS Swap (Constant Maturity Swap)

Receive the 10-year swap rate every quarter for 5 years — not a fixed rate, not SOFR, but the current long-term rate at each reset.

A CMS swap is like a regular swap with a twist: instead of one leg paying a fixed rate or overnight SOFR, one leg pays the prevailing n-year swap rate at each reset date. CMS stands for Constant Maturity Swap because the "maturity" of the underlying rate reference stays constant (always 10y, always 2y, etc.) even as time passes.

Start here — the key insight

In a vanilla 5y swap receiving fixed 4%, you lock in 4% for 5 years. In a 5y CMS10 swap receiving the 10y rate quarterly, you receive "whatever the 10y swap rate is" four times a year for 5 years. If 10y rates go to 6%, you receive 6%. If they go to 3%, you receive 3%. The CMS rate floats, but it floats with the long end of the curve, not with overnight rates.

The CMS spread X is set so the swap has zero value at inception. It is not zero — it embeds the convexity adjustment, the forward slope of the curve, and the vol premium. If the 10y rate is above SOFR + X, the CMS receiver profits.

Step by step — understanding the cash flows

Quarter 1: CMS10 resets to 4.30%

On Jan 1, the market 10y swap rate is 4.30%. This rate is fixed for the quarter. On Mar 31:

  • CMS receiver gets: 4.30% × $100M × 91/360 = $1,085,556
  • CMS payer gets: (SOFR avg + X) × $100M × 91/360 (e.g., if SOFR = 5.20%, X = −60bp → 4.60% → $1,161,111)
  • Net: CMS receiver pays $75,556 this quarter (10y is below funding)

Quarter 3: CMS10 resets to 5.80% (curve steepens)

Six months later, the curve has steepened dramatically:

  • CMS receiver gets: 5.80% × $100M × 91/360 = $1,463,889
  • CMS payer gets: (5.20% avg SOFR − 60bp = 4.60%) × $100M × 91/360 = $1,161,111
  • Net: CMS receiver receives $302,778 this quarter (long end has risen sharply)

The CMS receiver profits when the 10y rate is above their breakeven (SOFR + X). The product is essentially a bet that the long end of the curve will outperform SOFR — a steepener view expressed as a swap.

Who uses CMS and why

Insurance companies

Insurers have 20–30y liabilities. They want exposure to the 10y rate — high 10y rates mean more investment income. Receiving CMS10 for 10 years gives them floating exposure to the long end without committing to a 10y fixed rate. If rates rise, their CMS receipts rise too, matching their reinvestment opportunity.

Mortgage banks

Mortgage prepayment speeds correlate strongly with the 10y rate (low rates → refinancing wave). A bank receiving CMS10 hedges prepayment: when 10y rates fall (more prepayments, less income from mortgages), the CMS receipt also falls, but the bank receives SOFR + spread which may rise in a Fed-cutting environment.

Relative value traders

A CMS2 vs CMS10 spread swap receives the 10y rate and pays the 2y rate (or vice versa). This is a pure curve steepener/flattener with no outright duration — a clean bet on the 2s10s slope.

Structured product desks

Retail structured notes often pay "CMS10 × 5" or "CMS10 − CMS2 + 150bp." The desk hedges these exposures using CMS swaps and CMS caps/floors. Understanding CMS is essential to understanding what's inside many retail rates products.

The convexity adjustment — why fair CMS ≠ forward swap rate
This is the part most people get wrong

Intuition says: "The fair CMS10 rate for a payment in 2 years should equal the 2y forward 10y swap rate." That is wrong. The fair rate is higher. The difference — the convexity adjustment — can run 5–15bp depending on volatility and payment timing.

If you price a CMS product ignoring this adjustment, you will systematically give away money to whoever you trade with.

The Jensen's inequality argument

To see why, think about what the CMS receiver actually receives. At time T, they get the market 10y rate S_T. But the value of receiving S_T at time T is not just S_T — it's S_T discounted at the T-maturity discount factor P(0,T). The trouble is that S_T and P(0,T) are correlated: when S_T is high (rates high), P(0,T) is low (high rates → smaller discount factors). The payment is "cheap" when the rate is high, and "expensive" when the rate is low.

Concrete example: Suppose S_T can be 3% or 7% with equal probability (E[S_T] = 5%). Under the forward measure, the OIS forward rate F = 5%.

The CMS receiver receives S_T, discounted at the T-forward discount factor. The 10y annuity A(7%) ≈ 6.9y. The annuity A(3%) ≈ 8.5y. Under the T-forward measure, high-rate scenarios are over-weighted (because the annuity is smaller, the payment measure assigns more weight to that scenario). The result: ET[S_T] > 5%.

The fair CMS rate might be 5.08% — the receiver must be offered 8bp more than the forward swap rate to compensate for this convexity bias.

What drives the size of the adjustment?

Larger adjustment when:

  • Higher vol — more rate dispersion = more Jensen's effect. At 120bp vol vs 80bp vol, the adjustment roughly doubles.
  • Longer payment time — the further in the future the payment, the more vol can accumulate. Adjustment grows roughly as σ²×T.
  • Longer swap tenor — a longer underlying swap has a more curved annuity function. CMS30 has more adjustment than CMS5 for the same payment time.

In the swap market, the natural measure for pricing is the annuity measure QA, with numeraire A(S_T) (the present value of the swap annuity). Under QA, the forward swap rate is a martingale: EA[S_T] = F.

A CMS payment at time T is priced under the T-forward measure QT, with numeraire P(0,T). To compute ET[S_T], we change measure via the Radon-Nikodym derivative:

\[\mathbb{E}^T[S_T] = \frac{\mathbb{E}^A[S_T \cdot A(S_T)]}{\mathbb{E}^A[A(S_T)]}\]

Since A(S) is a decreasing function of S (when rates are high, the annuity is small), S and A(S) are negatively correlated. By Jensen's inequality applied to the covariance:

\[\mathbb{E}^A[S_T \cdot A(S_T)] > \mathbb{E}^A[S_T] \cdot \mathbb{E}^A[A(S_T)] = F \cdot \mathbb{E}^A[A(S_T)]\]

Therefore ET[S_T] > F. The adjustment in bp is computed numerically: assume S_T ~ N(F, σ_N²·T) under QA, then integrate S × A(S) × φ(S) and divide by ∫ A(S) × φ(S)dS. This is what the CMS calculator on the Convexity page computes.

A simple closed-form approximation (linear annuity model):

\[\text{CMS adj} \approx \sigma_N^2 \cdot T \cdot \frac{D_A}{1 + F \cdot D_A}\]

where D_A is the modified duration of the annuity (approximately the swap's modified duration) and σ_N is the normal vol in decimal (e.g., 0.008 for 80bp/√yr).

The CMS spread option — the most common CMS variant

A CMS spread option pays max(0, CMS10 − CMS2 − K) at expiry — essentially a call option on the 2s10s slope. It's zero-cost when the slope equals the strike K, and pays off if the curve steepens beyond K. Pricing requires not just CMS convexity adjustments for each tenor, but also the correlation between CMS10 and CMS2 rates. The correlation is typically 90–95%, meaning the spread has much lower vol than either leg alone.

Non-Linear

Swaption

The right, but not the obligation, to enter an interest rate swap at a future date at a pre-agreed fixed rate.

A swaption gives you optionality on rates. A payer swaption is the right to pay fixed (enter a payer swap) — it profits if rates rise above the strike. A receiver swaption is the right to receive fixed — it profits if rates fall below the strike. Swaptions are the primary way to buy or sell interest rate volatility.

Why it exists

A corporate treasurer knows they will issue a 10y bond in 6 months. They don't know what rates will be then, but they want protection against a spike. They buy a 6M × 10Y payer swaption (6-month expiry, 10-year underlying swap). If rates rise, they exercise — they enter a receive-fixed swap that offsets the higher funding cost on the bond. If rates fall, they let it lapse and benefit from the lower bond yield. The cost is the premium.

1Y × 10Y payer swaption — strike 4.50%, pay premium upfront
Today: Buyer pays ~120bp premium for the right to enter a 10y payer swap at 4.50% in 1 year.
In 1 year (rates at 5.20%): Buyer exercises. Enters 10y swap paying 4.50% vs receiving SOFR — immediately worth 70bp × 10y annuity ≈ 6.0% of notional.
In 1 year (rates at 3.80%): Buyer lets the swaption expire worthless. Net cost = 120bp premium.

Swaption matrix — how traders quote vol

Swaptions are identified by two dimensions: expiry (time until exercise) × tenor (length of the underlying swap). A "1×10" is a 1-year expiry on a 10-year swap. A "5×5" is 5-year expiry on a 5-year swap. Vol is quoted on this 2D matrix and the surface has a rich smile structure.

= N(d₁) × underlying swap DV01. ATM swaption delta ≈ 50%. Grows toward 100% as it goes deep ITM.
Gamma
Highest at ATM, near expiry. This is the "gamma premium" — what the option seller needs to be compensated for continuous delta-hedging cost.
Vega
Sensitivity to a 1bp rise in normal vol. A 1Y×10Y swaption on $100M: vega ≈ $5,000–10,000 per bp of vol.
Theta
Daily time decay. For a long option position, theta is always negative — you bleed premium each day you hold. Theta vs gamma is the key P&L trade-off.

The market standard for swaption pricing is the Bachelier model (normal model), where the forward swap rate follows arithmetic Brownian motion: dF = σ_N dW. This is preferred over Black's model (lognormal) because it allows negative rates and fits the observed vol smile better for rates.

\[V_{\text{payer}} = A \cdot \left[(F - K)\,\Phi(d) + \sigma_N\sqrt{T}\,\phi(d)\right], \quad d = \frac{F-K}{\sigma_N\sqrt{T}}\]

where A is the swap annuity, F is the forward swap rate, K is the strike, σ_N is the normal vol (in bp/√yr), T is expiry, and Φ, φ are the standard normal CDF and PDF. For a receiver swaption, swap F and K in the first term and flip the sign.

The "normal vol" σ_N is quoted in bp per square root of year. A vol of 80bp means the standard deviation of the rate move over 1 year is 80bp. For a 3-month option (T=0.25), the expected move is 80bp × √0.25 = 40bp. ATM swaption premium ≈ 40% × σ_N × √T × A.

European vs Bermudan — the exercise style matters enormously

A European swaption can only be exercised on one date. A Bermudan swaption can be exercised on any coupon date of the underlying swap — you can enter the swap early if rates move sharply. Bermudan swaptions are far more valuable (the optionality of early exercise is worth 20–40% of the European price for long-dated structures) but require a full interest rate model (Hull-White, LMM) to price, not just the Bachelier formula. Most corporate callable bonds embed a Bermudan receiver swaption.

Non-Linear

Interest Rate Cap & Floor

A strip of call or put options on the floating rate — one option per payment period.

An interest rate cap is a series of caplets, each of which pays max(0, SOFR − K) × notional × period for its respective period. A floor is the same but with put options (pays when SOFR < K). The cap buyer is protected against floating rates rising above K; the floor buyer is protected against rates falling below K.

Classic use case — floating-rate borrower buys a cap

A company borrows $100M at SOFR + 150bp. They're comfortable up to an all-in rate of 7%, so they buy a 5-year cap at K = 5.50% (which gets them to 7% all-in including their spread). Each quarter, if SOFR exceeds 5.50%, the cap pays the excess. The total premium is paid upfront (or can be amortised into a running premium via a swap overlay). The company has effectively bought a ceiling on their interest cost.

Cap vs Swaption — key difference

A swaption gives you one exercise decision — enter the swap or don't. A cap gives you 20 independent exercise decisions (one per quarter in a 5y cap). Each caplet exercises independently based on that period's SOFR. The cap therefore has more optionality than a swaption with the same strike and expiry — caps are more expensive than equivalent swaptions on a premium basis.

Put-call parity for rates

Cap − Floor = Swap (pay fixed at K). Buying a cap and selling a floor at the same strike K is equivalent to entering a payer swap at K. This is put-call parity for rates. It means: if you know the IRS par rate, you know the cap-floor relationship. You can check for arbitrage: if cap − floor ≠ swap value at the same strike, something is mispriced.

Delta
Sum of individual caplet deltas. An ATM cap has delta ~50% of the equivalent swap DV01. Delta grows as cap goes ITM.
Gamma
Each caplet contributes gamma independently. The near-expiry caplets have the highest gamma per $ premium (gamma near expiry is high). The gamma profile is distributed across all payment dates.
Vega
Sensitive to the short-end vol surface. Each caplet is priced using the caplet vol for that specific expiry — not the single swaption vol. The cap vega profile is a strip of individual vega terms.
Theta
As each caplet approaches its expiry, its theta increases (more rapid time decay). The total cap theta is the sum across all caplets — heavier near-dated caplets dominate.
The vol surface matters — caps use "caplet vols" not swaption vols

A 5y cap at 5.50% is 20 caplets. Each is priced with the vol for its specific expiry (3M vol for the first caplet, 6M vol for the second, …, 5Y vol for the last). These "caplet vols" or "spot vols" come from the cap vol surface, which is different from the swaption vol matrix. Stripping caplet vols from quoted cap prices requires bootstrapping. The two vol surfaces are related but not identical — they encode different market expectations about the distribution of rates at different horizons.
