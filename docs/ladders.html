Delta Ladder

The Delta Ladder

A rates book has hundreds of instruments. Without bucketing sensitivity by tenor, you can't tell if your net $50k DV01 is one big 30Y bond risk or a dozen mismatches across the curve — and those are completely different problems.

Why Traders Need This — Not Just Net DV01

A single net DV01 number is nearly useless. It tells you what happens if rates move in parallel. They never do. The 2Y/10Y spread moves independently. The belly (5Y) has its own supply/demand dynamics. A "flat" book with $200k long at 2Y and $195k short at 10Y looks like $5k net — but a 15bp flattening wipes $3M. The delta ladder makes that visible. Net DV01 hides it.

What Goes Into the Delta Ladder

  • Notional × Modified Duration × 0.0001 — the DV01 formula for bonds and swaps
  • Sign: receive-fixed swap or long bond = positive DV01 (you profit when rates fall). Pay-fixed or short bond = negative.
  • Tenor bucket assignment: a 5Y swap distributes ~90% of its DV01 to the 5Y bucket; small residuals sit in shorter buckets at each coupon date
  • Net vs Gross: net = long minus short per bucket. Gross shows total long and total short separately — a gross/net ratio above 5x is a warning sign

Deep Dive: Key Rate DV01 vs Parallel DV01

A parallel DV01 shifts all tenor rates by 1bp simultaneously — useful for a quick portfolio summary but misleading for curve risk. A key rate DV01 (also called key rate duration or KRD) shifts only one tenor node by 1bp while holding all others fixed.

Example: a 5Y pay-fixed swap has a key rate DV01 of roughly −$45k at the 5Y point. But it also has small positive exposures at 1Y, 2Y, 3Y, 4Y from the floating leg resets. Summing all key rate DV01s equals the parallel DV01.

The delta ladder stacks key rate DV01s by bucket. A flat net DV01 row in the ladder means that specific point on the curve is hedged. A flat total means the book is parallel-shift neutral — but the individual rows tell you whether you're exposed to curve steepening, flattening, or belly moves.

Gamma Ladder

The Gamma Ladder

The delta ladder is a snapshot — it tells you your risk at this moment. The gamma ladder tells you where that snapshot is going the moment rates start moving.

The Hidden Risk in a Flat Delta Ladder

You sell a 5Y swaption to collect premium. You delta-hedge it immediately — your delta ladder goes back to flat. Perfect. But your gamma is now −$3,500/bp² at 5Y. When the ECB surprises and 5Y rates spike 40bp, your delta drifts to −$140k short. To re-flatten, you must buy $140k of 5Y DV01 — in a market that just moved 40bp against you. The gamma ladder showed this risk before the trade. The delta ladder showed nothing.

What Goes Into the Gamma Ladder

  • Options positions (swaptions, caps, floors): the only instruments with meaningful gamma. Linear instruments (swaps, bonds, futures) have negligible gamma.
  • Notional and strike: ATM options carry the highest gamma. Deep in- or out-of-the-money options have less.
  • Time to expiry: shorter-dated options have higher gamma — the delta changes faster as they approach a binary outcome.
  • Implied volatility: higher vol → slightly lower gamma (option already prices large moves as probable).
  • Long vs short: buying options = positive gamma (convexity benefit). Selling = negative gamma (re-hedging risk).

Deep Dive: The Gamma P&L Formula and Break-Even Vol

For a position with delta Δ (in DV01 terms) and gamma Γ at a given tenor, a rate move ΔR produces:

P&L ≈ Δ × ΔR + ½ × Γ × (ΔR)²

The second term is the gamma P&L. For long gamma (Γ > 0), this is always positive — you profit whether rates go up or down. For short gamma (Γ < 0), you lose on both sides.

But long gamma isn't free. You pay theta — time decay — every day to hold the option. The break-even daily rate move is:

Break-even vol = √(2 × Theta / Gamma)

Example: long 5Y straddle costs $50k theta/day with $2,000/bp² gamma. Break-even = √(2 × 50,000 / 2,000) = 7.1 bp/day. If realised vol in the 5Y rate exceeds 7.1bp/day on average over the option's life, the trade is profitable.

Delta re-hedging: after a 30bp move with Γ = +$2,000/bp², your 5Y DV01 shifts by 2,000 × 30 = $60,000. You sell $60k DV01 of 5Y bonds to re-flatten. That re-hedging trade at the new level locks in the gamma P&L — this is why long gamma positions get re-hedged frequently.

Combined View

Reading Them Together

A flat delta ladder is not a flat risk. The gamma ladder tells you whether that flatness survives the next 30bp move — and what it costs to restore it.

What Traders Actually Ask Every Morning

Before any central bank event or data release: (1) Net delta per bucket — am I positioned for the move I expect? (2) Net gamma per bucket — if I'm wrong about direction, how badly does my delta drift? (3) Theta vs expected gamma P&L — is the option premium worth paying given implied vs realised vol? The delta ladder answers (1). The gamma ladder answers (2) and enables (3).

The Four Quadrants of Risk

Long Delta + Long Gamma
Positioned for a rally and get more long if rates fall. The bigger the move down, the better. Classic risk-on with convexity: even if wrong on direction, gamma limits the loss.
Long Delta + Short Gamma
Positioned for a rally but sold options to monetise vol. A moderate rally is great. A violent rally less so — your delta shrinks as rates fall. If wrong, losses accelerate. Only works if vol is rich.
Short Delta + Long Gamma
Positioned for a sell-off with convexity protection. A sharp sell-off is very profitable; so is a big rally (gamma compensates). The cost is theta every day while waiting for the move.
Short Delta + Short Gamma
The most exposed quadrant. Positioned for a sell-off, but if wrong the losses accelerate as rates fall. Requires constant monitoring, tight stops, and is only used when vol is significantly overpriced.

The Belly Butterfly
Long 5Y delta (+$95k DV01), short 2Y and 10Y equally (−$45k each). Net roughly zero, positioned for the 5Y belly to outperform. Add a long 5Y straddle for gamma: if rates move more than expected, the convexity P&L offsets the butterfly cost. The Duration Hedge Mismatch
An asset manager holds $2B in long-dated bonds: +$160k DV01 at 20Y, +$120k at 30Y. They hedge with 10Y futures to cut duration by half. Net DV01 now looks flat. But the delta ladder shows they're long at 20Y–30Y and short at 10Y — a pure curve flattener bet they didn't intend to make. The "hedged" portfolio still has $280k of curve risk.
+$160k 20Y +$120k 30Y −$280k 10Y
🎯
The Break-Even Vol Trade
Before buying a 3M5Y straddle, a vol trader computes: theta = $50k/day, gamma = $2,000/bp². Break-even daily move = √(2 × 50,000 / 2,000) = 7.1 bp/day. Trailing 30-day realised vol in 5Y rates is 9bp/day. Implied vol looks cheap. The gamma ladder makes this arithmetic explicit: every tenor where gamma > break-even vol is a buy; every tenor where it's below is a sell.

Reference

Glossary

DV01
Dollar Value of a Basis Point — the P&L for a 1bp (0.01%) move in rates. The primary unit of delta risk for all rates instruments. Units: $/bp.
Delta Ladder
A chart or table of DV01 values bucketed by tenor (3M, 1Y, 5Y, 10Y, etc.). Shows where rate sensitivity is concentrated across the yield curve.
Gamma Ladder
Rate of change of DV01 per basis point, bucketed by tenor. Units: $/bp². Tells you how fast your delta ladder changes when rates move.
Key Rate DV01
DV01 from shifting only one tenor node (e.g. 5Y) by 1bp while holding all others fixed. Reveals curve-specific risk that parallel DV01 hides entirely.
Tenor Bucket
A maturity point (e.g. "5Y bucket") to which rate sensitivity is assigned. Multiple instruments' DV01s are summed within each bucket to show net exposure at that point.
Net vs Gross DV01
Net = long minus short per bucket. Gross = total long + total short. A net-zero bucket can still hide $500k of offsetting positions. Gross/net > 5× is a red flag.
Positive Gamma
Long convexity — your delta increases as rates fall (you get longer in rallies) and decreases as rates rise. Comes from buying options. Always costs theta.
Negative Gamma
Short convexity — your delta moves the wrong way after a rate move. From selling options. Forces you to buy when markets are rising and sell when falling — a systematic loss in volatile markets.
Theta
Daily time-decay cost of long options positions, in dollars. Long gamma costs theta daily; short gamma earns it. The theta/gamma ratio determines break-even volatility.
Re-hedging
Buying or selling delta after a rate move to restore a flat delta ladder. Short-gamma books must re-hedge frequently — buying high and selling low in volatile markets — generating a systematic P&L drag.
Break-even Vol
The daily rate move at which long-gamma P&L equals theta cost: √(2 × Theta / Gamma). If realised vol exceeds this, long gamma wins. Used to decide whether to buy or sell options at current implied vol.
Convexity
The second derivative of bond price with respect to yield — equivalent to gamma for bonds. Positive convexity means you gain more when rates fall than you lose when rates rise by the same amount.
