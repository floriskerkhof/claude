Foundation

How DV01 is Calculated

DV01 is the dollar change in a position's value when its yield moves by exactly 1 basis point (0.01%). Understanding where the number comes from makes every figure in the ladder concrete rather than abstract.

Start Here — The Intuition Before Any Formula

Imagine you own a bond that pays you $4M per year (4% on $100M face value) for 5 years, then returns your $100M. You paid $100M for it today because 4% is exactly the market rate — a fair deal.

Now the market moves and new bonds yield 4.01% (one basis point more). Your bond is now slightly unattractive — it still pays 4%, but someone could get 4.01% elsewhere. To sell your bond, you'd need to drop the price slightly. That price drop is your DV01: roughly $43,000 on a $100M, 5Y bond.

The key driver is time. A 1bp change in yield affects every future cash flow — but cash flows far in the future are affected much more than near-term ones, because the discount factor compounds over more periods. That's why a 30Y bond has a DV01 roughly 5× larger than a 5Y bond with the same notional: it has far more future cash flows sitting at the end of a long discount chain.

Why Bond Prices Fall When Yields Rise

This trips up almost everyone at first. Think of it this way: the bond's coupon is fixed at the time of issue. If market rates then rise, the bond's fixed coupon looks less attractive — it's paying below the new market rate. So buyers will only buy it at a discount. The higher the yield rise, the bigger the discount needed. That's the inverse relationship.

Mathematically: the price of a bond is the present value of all its future cash flows. Present value means discounting: a cash flow of $1 received in 5 years is worth less than $1 today, because you could have invested $1 today and earned interest. The discount rate is the yield. A higher yield → heavier discounting → lower present values → lower price.

DV01 is simply how much the total present value changes when the yield moves 1bp. The calculator below shows this step by step.

Deep Dive: Macaulay Duration, Modified Duration, and Why They're Different

Macaulay Duration: the weighted average waiting time

Imagine a 5Y bond pays you 10 semiannual coupons plus the principal at the end. Some of that money arrives in 6 months, some in 1 year, some in 5 years. Macaulay Duration asks: on average, when do you get paid? It weights each cash flow by its present value, then averages the timing. The result is always less than the bond's maturity — because coupons arrive before maturity and pull the average forward. A zero-coupon bond has Macaulay Duration = maturity exactly, because all cash flow arrives at the end.

Why does this matter? Bonds with longer Macaulay Duration are more sensitive to yield changes, because more of their value is sitting far out in the future where discount factors magnify the rate change.

Modified Duration: the direct price-sensitivity measure

Macaulay Duration is elegant but you can't directly multiply it by a yield change to get a price change. You need a small adjustment:

Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration / (1 + yield / m)

where m is the compounding frequency (2 for semiannual). For a 5Y bond at 4% yield: Modified Duration ≈ Macaulay / 1.02. The difference is small but matters for precision. Modified Duration tells you the percentage change in price for a 100bp (1%) change in yield. So if Modified Duration = 4.5 years and yields rise 1%, the bond loses approximately 4.5% of its value.

From Modified Duration to DV01

DV01 is just Modified Duration scaled to a 1bp (0.01%) move instead of 100bp:

DV01 = Price × Modified Duration × 0.0001

Example: $100M bond, Modified Duration = 4.5 years. DV01 = $100,000,000 × 4.5 × 0.0001 = $45,000. Every basis point move costs or earns $45,000.

Why DV01 is not constant: convexity

DV01 assumes a linear relationship between yield and price. In reality, for large moves, the relationship curves — a 100bp fall in yields gains slightly more than a 100bp rise in yields loses. This curvature is called convexity and it's why the gamma ladder matters: gamma is the second-order correction to the DV01 approximation.

For a receive-fixed interest rate swap

A swap has no upfront cash exchange — only a series of future net payments (fixed minus floating). The floating leg resets to market rates at each period start, which means it always reprices to par immediately after each reset. Its "duration" is approximately the time to the next reset — nearly zero for frequent resets. So the whole swap's rate sensitivity comes from the fixed leg, making receive-fixed swap DV01 ≈ equivalent fixed-rate bond DV01. This is why traders treat swaps and bonds interchangeably when building the delta ladder.

Bump-and-reprice: the universal check

For any instrument, you can always verify DV01 numerically without the analytical formula:

DV01 = [ Price(yield − 0.5bp) − Price(yield + 0.5bp) ] / 2

Shift the yield down 0.5bp, reprice. Shift it up 0.5bp, reprice. The average dollar difference is your DV01. Trading systems use this for complex derivatives where analytical formulas become intractable. The calculator above shows this verification for the current inputs.

Delta Ladder

The Delta Ladder

A rates book has hundreds of instruments. Without bucketing sensitivity by tenor, you can't tell if your net $50k DV01 is one big 30Y bond risk or a dozen mismatches across the curve — and those are completely different problems.

Why Traders Need This — Not Just Net DV01

A single net DV01 number is nearly useless. It tells you what happens if rates move in parallel. They never do. The 2Y/10Y spread moves independently. The belly (5Y) has its own supply/demand dynamics. A "flat" book with $200k long at 2Y and $195k short at 10Y looks like $5k net — but a 15bp flattening wipes $3M. The delta ladder makes that visible. Net DV01 hides it.

What Goes Into the Delta Ladder

  • Notional × Modified Duration × 0.0001 — the DV01 formula for bonds and swaps
  • Sign: receive-fixed swap or long bond = positive DV01 (you profit when rates fall). Pay-fixed or short bond = negative.
  • Tenor bucket assignment: a 5Y swap distributes ~90% of its DV01 to the 5Y bucket; small residuals sit in shorter buckets at each coupon date
  • Net vs Gross: net = long minus short per bucket. Gross shows total long and total short separately — a gross/net ratio above 5x is a warning sign

Deep Dive: Key Rate DV01 vs Parallel DV01

A parallel DV01 shifts all tenor rates by 1bp simultaneously — useful for a quick portfolio summary but misleading for curve risk. A key rate DV01 (also called key rate duration or KRD) shifts only one tenor node by 1bp while holding all others fixed.

Example: a 5Y pay-fixed swap has a key rate DV01 of roughly −$45k at the 5Y point. But it also has small positive exposures at 1Y, 2Y, 3Y, 4Y from the floating leg resets. Summing all key rate DV01s equals the parallel DV01.

The delta ladder stacks key rate DV01s by bucket. A flat net DV01 row in the ladder means that specific point on the curve is hedged. A flat total means the book is parallel-shift neutral — but the individual rows tell you whether you're exposed to curve steepening, flattening, or belly moves.

Gamma Ladder

The Gamma Ladder

The delta ladder is a snapshot — it tells you your risk at this moment. The gamma ladder tells you where that snapshot is going the moment rates start moving.

The Hidden Risk in a Flat Delta Ladder

You sell a 5Y swaption to collect premium. You delta-hedge it immediately — your delta ladder goes back to flat. Perfect. But your gamma is now −$3,500/bp² at 5Y. When the ECB surprises and 5Y rates spike 40bp, your delta drifts to −$140k short. To re-flatten, you must buy $140k of 5Y DV01 — in a market that just moved 40bp against you. The gamma ladder showed this risk before the trade. The delta ladder showed nothing.

What Goes Into the Gamma Ladder

  • Options positions (swaptions, caps, floors): the only instruments with meaningful gamma. Linear instruments (swaps, bonds, futures) have negligible gamma.
  • Notional and strike: ATM options carry the highest gamma. Deep in- or out-of-the-money options have less.
  • Time to expiry: shorter-dated options have higher gamma — the delta changes faster as they approach a binary outcome.
  • Implied volatility: higher vol → slightly lower gamma (option already prices large moves as probable).
  • Long vs short: buying options = positive gamma (convexity benefit). Selling = negative gamma (re-hedging risk).

Deep Dive: The Gamma P&L Formula and Break-Even Vol

For a position with delta Δ (in DV01 terms) and gamma Γ at a given tenor, a rate move ΔR produces:

P&L ≈ Δ × ΔR + ½ × Γ × (ΔR)²

The second term is the gamma P&L. For long gamma (Γ > 0), this is always positive — you profit whether rates go up or down. For short gamma (Γ < 0), you lose on both sides.

But long gamma isn't free. You pay theta — time decay — every day to hold the option. The break-even daily rate move is:

Break-even vol = √(2 × Theta / Gamma)

Example: long 5Y straddle costs $50k theta/day with $2,000/bp² gamma. Break-even = √(2 × 50,000 / 2,000) = 7.1 bp/day. If realised vol in the 5Y rate exceeds 7.1bp/day on average over the option's life, the trade is profitable.

Delta re-hedging: after a 30bp move with Γ = +$2,000/bp², your 5Y DV01 shifts by 2,000 × 30 = $60,000. You sell $60k DV01 of 5Y bonds to re-flatten. That re-hedging trade at the new level locks in the gamma P&L — this is why long gamma positions get re-hedged frequently.

Combined View

Reading Them Together

A flat delta ladder is not a flat risk. The gamma ladder tells you whether that flatness survives the next 30bp move — and what it costs to restore it.

What Traders Actually Ask Every Morning

Before any central bank event or data release: (1) Net delta per bucket — am I positioned for the move I expect? (2) Net gamma per bucket — if I'm wrong about direction, how badly does my delta drift? (3) Theta vs expected gamma P&L — is the option premium worth paying given implied vs realised vol? The delta ladder answers (1). The gamma ladder answers (2) and enables (3).

The Four Quadrants of Risk

All examples below use a 5Y position with DV01 = $44k/bp and gamma = ±$2,000/bp². These are realistic numbers for a $100M notional position.

Long Delta + Long Gamma
Positioned for a rally. The bigger the move down in rates, the more profitable you become — gamma accelerates gains. Even if wrong (rates rise), gamma cushions the loss.
Rates fall 20bp:
  Delta P&L: +$44k × 20 = +$880k
  Gamma P&L: ½ × $2,000 × 20² = +$400k
  Total: +$1,280k · new DV01: +$84k

Rates rise 20bp (wrong way):
  Delta P&L: −$880k
  Gamma P&L: +$400k (always positive)
  Total: −$480k instead of −$880k ✓
Long Delta + Short Gamma
Positioned for a rally but sold options to earn premium. A moderate rally is great. A large move in either direction hurts — gamma bleeds away your delta or accelerates losses.
Rates fall 20bp:
  Delta P&L: +$880k
  Gamma P&L: ½ × (−$2,000) × 20² = −$400k
  Total: +$480k · new DV01: +$4k only

Rates rise 20bp (wrong way):
  Delta P&L: −$880k
  Gamma P&L: −$400k
  Total: −$1,280k · losses accelerate ✗
Short Delta + Long Gamma
Positioned for a sell-off with convexity protection. A sharp sell-off is very profitable. Even a big rally pays off via gamma. The daily cost is theta — you pay to hold this profile.
Rates rise 30bp (right way):
  Delta P&L: +$1,320k
  Gamma P&L: ½ × $2,000 × 30² = +$900k
  Total: +$2,220k

Rates fall 30bp (wrong way):
  Delta P&L: −$1,320k
  Gamma P&L: +$900k
  Total: −$420k — gamma saved $900k ✓
Short Delta + Short Gamma
The most dangerous quadrant. Right-way moves give muted gains. Wrong-way moves produce accelerating losses. Only viable when implied vol is very rich relative to realised vol.

Net DV01 per bucket Σ(Notional × Duration × 0.0001)
Your rate sensitivity at each tenor — how many dollars you make or lose for every 1bp move at that specific point on the curve. This is the primary number in the delta ladder.
Example: $100M notional, 5Y bond, Modified Duration = 4.41 years
DV01 = $100,000,000 × 4.41 × 0.0001 = $44,100 per bp
 
This $44,100 sits in the 5Y bucket.
10bp sell-off in 5Y rates → you lose $44,100 × 10 = $441,000
100bp sell-off → you lose $4,410,000
⚠ Trigger: any bucket breaches risk limits
Gross/Net DV01 ratio Σ|DV01| / |Σ(DV01)|
Detects hidden size in a "flat" book. A net-zero bucket can still hide enormous offsetting positions that move against you when the curve shifts non-uniformly.
2Y bucket: +$200,000 DV01 (receive-fixed swaps)
10Y bucket: −$195,000 DV01 (pay-fixed swaps)
 
Net DV01 = $200k − $195k = $5,000 — looks flat
Gross DV01 = $200k + $195k = $395,000
Ratio = $395k / $5k = 79× ← massive warning
 
A 15bp curve flattening (2Y up, 10Y flat):
Loss = $200,000 × 15 = $3,000,000 — the $5k net told you nothing
⚠ Trigger: ratio above 5× warrants investigation
Gamma / DV01 Γ / DV01 per bucket
How fast your delta changes relative to its current size. A high ratio means your hedge will be meaningfully wrong after even a small rate move — you'll need to re-hedge frequently.
5Y DV01 = +$44,000  |  5Y gamma = −$2,000/bp²
Ratio = $2,000 / $44,000 = 4.5% per bp
 
Interpretation: for every 1bp move in 5Y rates,
your 5Y delta changes by 4.5% of its current size.
 
After a 10bp move: delta changes by 4.5% × 10 = 45% — you
need to re-hedge nearly half your position
After a 22bp move: delta is fully gone (100% change)
⚠ Trigger: above 5%/bp means active re-hedging required
Break-even volatility √(2 × Theta / Gamma)
For a long-gamma position: the average daily rate move you need to cover the theta you pay each day. If realised vol exceeds this, long gamma is profitable. Below it, you're paying more in theta than you earn from re-hedging.
Long 5Y straddle: theta = $30,000/day  |  gamma = +$1,500/bp²
Break-even = √(2 × $30,000 / $1,500) = √40 = 6.3 bp/day
 
If 5Y rates move 4 bp/day on average (below break-even):
  Gamma P&L/day: ½ × $1,500 × 4² = $12,000
  Theta cost/day: $30,000 → net −$18,000/day ✗
 
If 5Y rates move 9 bp/day (above break-even):
  Gamma P&L/day: ½ × $1,500 × 9² = $60,750
  Theta cost/day: $30,000 → net +$30,750/day ✓
⚠ Compare to trailing 20-day realised vol before buying options
Gamma P&L at scenario ½ × Γ × (ΔR)²
The convexity P&L from a specific rate shock — independent of delta. For long gamma this is the "free money" on a big move. For short gamma it's the hidden loss that compounds on top of any delta P&L.
Short gamma −$2,000/bp² in 5Y bucket. ECB surprises +40bp.
 
Gamma P&L = ½ × (−$2,000) × 40² = ½ × (−$2,000) × 1,600
          = −$1,600,000
 
This loss is on top of any delta P&L and re-hedging costs.

The Duration Hedge Mismatch
An asset manager holds $2B in long-dated bonds: +$160k DV01 at 20Y, +$120k at 30Y. They hedge with 10Y futures to cut duration by half. Net DV01 now looks flat. But the delta ladder shows they're long at 20Y–30Y and short at 10Y — a pure curve flattener bet they didn't intend to make. The "hedged" portfolio still has $280k of curve risk.

The Break-Even Vol Trade
Before buying a 3M5Y straddle, a vol trader computes: theta = $50k/day, gamma = $2,000/bp². Break-even daily move = √(2 × 50,000 / 2,000) = 7.1 bp/day. Trailing 30-day realised vol in 5Y rates is 9bp/day. Implied vol looks cheap. The gamma ladder makes this arithmetic explicit: every tenor where gamma > break-even vol is a buy; every tenor where it's below is a sell.

Reference

Glossary

DV01
Dollar Value of a Basis Point — the P&L for a 1bp (0.01%) move in rates. The primary unit of delta risk for all rates instruments. Units: $/bp.
Delta Ladder
A chart or table of DV01 values bucketed by tenor (3M, 1Y, 5Y, 10Y, etc.). Shows where rate sensitivity is concentrated across the yield curve.
Gamma Ladder
Rate of change of DV01 per basis point, bucketed by tenor. Units: $/bp². Tells you how fast your delta ladder changes when rates move.
Key Rate DV01
DV01 from shifting only one tenor node (e.g. 5Y) by 1bp while holding all others fixed. Reveals curve-specific risk that parallel DV01 hides entirely.
Tenor Bucket
A maturity point (e.g. "5Y bucket") to which rate sensitivity is assigned. Multiple instruments' DV01s are summed within each bucket to show net exposure at that point.
Net vs Gross DV01
Net = long minus short per bucket. Gross = total long + total short. A net-zero bucket can still hide $500k of offsetting positions. Gross/net > 5× is a red flag.
Positive Gamma
Long convexity — your delta increases as rates fall (you get longer in rallies) and decreases as rates rise. Comes from buying options. Always costs theta.
Negative Gamma
Short convexity — your delta moves the wrong way after a rate move. From selling options. Forces you to buy when markets are rising and sell when falling — a systematic loss in volatile markets.
Theta
Daily time-decay cost of long options positions, in dollars. Long gamma costs theta daily; short gamma earns it. The theta/gamma ratio determines break-even volatility.
Re-hedging
Buying or selling delta after a rate move to restore a flat delta ladder. Short-gamma books must re-hedge frequently — buying high and selling low in volatile markets — generating a systematic P&L drag.
Break-even Vol
The daily rate move at which long-gamma P&L equals theta cost: √(2 × Theta / Gamma). If realised vol exceeds this, long gamma wins. Used to decide whether to buy or sell options at current implied vol.
Convexity
The second derivative of bond price with respect to yield — equivalent to gamma for bonds. Positive convexity means you gain more when rates fall than you lose when rates rise by the same amount.
