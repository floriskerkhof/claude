Glossary

Blue note
A flattened 3rd, 5th, or 7th note used in blues and jazz to create emotional tension not found in standard Western scales.
Backbeat
Accent on beats 2 and 4 of a 4/4 bar — the defining pulse of rock and soul. Practically invented popular music as we know it.
Modal jazz
Improvising over scales (modes) rather than chord progressions. Miles Davis's Kind of Blue freed jazz from bebop's harmonic complexity.
Ostinato
A persistently repeated musical figure — the riff in rock, the vamp in jazz. Gives minimalist music its hypnotic power.
Overdrive / Distortion
Intentional clipping of an audio signal, turning clean guitar tone into the sound of rock and roll. Pioneered by accident and then embraced obsessively.
Polyphony
Multiple independent melodic lines sounding simultaneously. Bach's fugues are the supreme example; he could weave five independent voices into a single coherent structure.
Counterpoint
The art of combining distinct melodic lines that are harmonically interdependent. The foundational technique of Western classical composition.
Tritone substitution
Replacing a dominant chord with a chord whose root is a tritone away. The sleek harmonic movement in bebop that gives jazz its sophisticated tension-and-release.
Dynamics
The range between quiet and loud in a performance. Classical music exploits dynamics over a span of 60dB; most modern pop has had it compressed out entirely.
Call and response
A musical conversation between two voices or sections — rooted in West African music, brought into gospel, blues, and soul, and the backbone of rock riffing.
Syncopation
Stressing off-beats or unexpected rhythmic positions. Syncopation is what makes jazz, funk, and reggae feel like they swing rather than march.
Tone poem
An orchestral work that tells a story or evokes a scene without words. Debussy's La Mer describes the ocean; you can almost hear the waves.
