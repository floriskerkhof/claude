Convexity

Your 30y bond rallied 3.2 points on a 50bp move. Your DV01 said 2.8. The extra $400k per $100M isn't luck — it's convexity, and your hedge ratio is already wrong.

The problem every rates desk faces after a big move

You're short duration via a 10y Treasury hedge. Rates rally 75bp. Your risk system said you'd lose $X on the short — but you lose more. The bond you sold short gained more value than DV01 predicted, because price and yield don't move in a straight line.

This is convexity: the curvature of the price/yield relationship. Long bonds are always long convexity — they outperform linear estimates in both rallies and selloffs. For swaps, the story is similar but the numbers differ. For CMS products and SOFR futures, convexity creates a systematic pricing adjustment that can run to several basis points.

This tool lets you see convexity in action, quantify it for bonds and swaps, and understand the CMS and futures adjustments that flow from the same mathematics.

Bond Convexity

Your DV01 tells you what happens on a 1bp move. But after a 50bp or 100bp shock, the linear estimate is wrong — the price/yield curve bends away from the tangent line in your favour.

Why a trader cares: You're long $100M of 10y Treasuries hedged with a DV01-matched short in 2y notes. Rates rally 100bp in a crisis. You expect to be flat. Instead, you make money — because the 10y bond's convexity far exceeds the 2y note's convexity. The hedge ratio was right at inception but is now wrong. This is the gamma of a bond book.

Convexity also determines relative value: two bonds with the same DV01 can have very different convexity. The higher-convexity bond is worth more — it outperforms in big moves in either direction. That's why it typically trades at a lower yield (higher price). Traders pay for convexity.

For a semi-annual coupon bond, the price as a function of yield is:

\[P(y) = \sum_{i=1}^{2T} \frac{C/2}{(1+y/2)^i} + \frac{100}{(1+y/2)^{2T}}\]

This is a convex function of y — the second derivative is always positive for a normal bond. Modified duration \(D_{mod}\) is the (negative) logarithmic first derivative:

\[D_{mod} = -\frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dy}, \quad \text{DV01} = D_{mod} \times P \times 0.0001\]

Convexity is the normalised second derivative:

\[C = \frac{1}{P}\frac{d^2P}{dy^2} = \frac{1}{P(1+y/2)^2}\sum_{i=1}^{2T} \frac{t_i(t_i+1)\cdot CF_i}{(1+y/2)^{t_i}}\]

where \(t_i = i/2\) is the time in years of the \(i\)-th cash flow. The Taylor approximation then gives:

\[\Delta P \approx -D_{mod} \cdot P \cdot \Delta y + \tfrac{1}{2} C \cdot P \cdot (\Delta y)^2\]

The convexity term grows with the square of the move. A 100bp shock has 4× the convexity benefit of a 50bp shock. This is why large rate moves matter so much for long-convexity positions.

Dollar convexity (used in PnL attribution) = \(\tfrac{1}{2} \times C \times P \times N \times (\Delta y)^2\) where \(N\) is notional and \(\Delta y\) is in decimal.

Swap Convexity

A vanilla interest rate swap has no coupon reinvestment — only cash flows on fixed and floating legs. Yet its DV01 still changes with rates, because the annuity factor (the present value of 1bp per period) shrinks when rates rise and grows when rates fall.

The hedge ratio problem: You're receiving fixed on a $100M 10y swap, hedged short duration with a 10y Treasury. Today they have the same DV01 — $8,700/bp each. After a 100bp rally, the swap's DV01 has grown to ~$9,400/bp while the bond's DV01 is ~$9,800/bp. The hedge has drifted. To stay flat you'd need to sell more bonds or buy back some of the swap.

Receiver swaps and long bonds both benefit from rate falls (positive convexity), but the convexity of a swap is slightly lower than an equivalent-duration bond because a bond's coupons create additional duration-shortening as yields change. For gamma trading purposes, options on swaps (swaptions) deliver pure convexity — vanilla swaps deliver it incidentally.

A swap's fixed-leg PV01 (the "annuity") is the present value of 1bp per period across all payment dates:

\[A(R) = \sum_{i=1}^{2T} \frac{\tau_i}{(1+R/2)^i}\]

where \(\tau_i = 0.5\) for semi-annual payments and \(R\) is the par swap rate. For a par receiver swap (fixed rate = par rate), the DV01 per unit notional is:

\[\text{DV01} = A(R) \times 0.0001\]

The rate sensitivity of DV01 (the "gamma" of the swap) is:

\[\frac{d(\text{DV01})}{dR} = \frac{dA}{dR} \times 0.0001 = -D_A \cdot A(R) \times 0.0001\]

where \(D_A\) is the modified duration of the annuity (roughly equal to the swap's modified duration). Since \(D_A > 0\), DV01 decreases as rates rise. For a 10y swap at 4%, \(D_A \approx 4.4\), so a 100bp rise in rates reduces DV01 by ~4.4%.

The convexity of a swap is positive (like a bond) but typically 10–15% lower than an equivalent-tenor bond because the bond's coupon payments shorten effective duration faster as yields rise.

CMS Convexity Adjustment

A CMS (Constant Maturity Swap) pays the n-year swap rate at a future date. The fair value of that payment is not the forward swap rate — it's higher, because of a convexity bias that can run 5–15bp on typical structures.

The CMS pricing problem: You're quoting a CMS swap where you receive the 10y swap rate in 2 years. The 2y forward 10y rate is 4.20%. Can you offer your client 4.20%? No — you need to offer more, maybe 4.27%, because of the convexity adjustment. If you don't add it, you give away 7bp of carry per year on the trade.

The adjustment arises from Jensen's inequality: you're receiving S × A(S) discounted at P(T). When S is high, A(S) is small (the annuity shrinks). When S is low, A(S) is large. This negative correlation between the rate and its "weight" means the expected receipt under the payment measure is higher than the forward swap rate. The effect grows with vol, payment time, and swap tenor.

The CMS payment at time \(T\) has value \(V = P(0,T) \cdot \mathbb{E}^T[S_T]\) where \(\mathbb{E}^T\) is the \(T\)-forward measure expectation. Under the annuity measure \(\mathbb{Q}^A\) (natural for swaps), \(\mathbb{E}^A[S_T] = F\) — the forward swap rate. Changing to the \(T\)-forward measure via the Radon-Nikodym derivative:

\[\mathbb{E}^T[S_T] = \frac{\mathbb{E}^A[S_T \cdot A(S_T)]}{\mathbb{E}^A[A(S_T)]}\]

Since \(A(S)\) decreases as \(S\) increases (higher rates → smaller annuity), \(S\) and \(A(S)\) are negatively correlated. By Jensen's inequality, \(\mathbb{E}^T[S_T] > F\). This is the convexity adjustment.

A simple closed-form approximation (assuming normal rates):

\[\text{CMS adj} \approx \sigma_N^2 \cdot T \cdot \frac{D_A}{1 + F \cdot D_A}\]

where \(\sigma_N\) is the normal vol (in decimal), \(T\) the payment time, and \(D_A\) the modified duration of the swap annuity. This tool computes it numerically by integrating over the full rate distribution, which is more accurate for large moves.

Futures Convexity Adjustment

SOFR futures (and their predecessors, Eurodollar futures) are not equivalent to FRAs. Daily margin settlement means a long futures position profits immediately when rates rise — making futures worth slightly less than the equivalent forward. The difference is the convexity adjustment.

Why it matters for curve building: When you strip a forward rate curve from SOFR futures, every contract needs a convexity adjustment. A 1y futures rate might be 4.800%, but the true OIS forward rate is 4.802%. The 5y futures is 4.200%, true OIS is 4.213%. If you skip the adjustment, your forward curve is systematically wrong, and any derivative you price off it will be mispriced.

The Hull (1993) formula gives: adjustment = ½ × σ² × T₁ × T₂, where T₁ is expiry and T₂ is the end of the reference period. At 100bp annual vol, a 5y contract has a 1.3bp adjustment; a 10y contract has a 5.25bp adjustment. These are small but systematic — they accumulate across the strip.

The daily mark-to-market of a futures contract creates a financing correlation: when rates rise, the long futures position receives variation margin which can be reinvested at the now-higher rate. When rates fall, the margin call must be funded at the now-lower rate. This asymmetry makes futures worth slightly less than a matching FRA.

Under Hull-White interest rate dynamics, the convexity adjustment for a futures contract on a rate observed over \([T_1, T_2]\) is:

\[\text{Futures rate} = \text{Forward rate} - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 T_1 T_2\]

where \(\sigma\) is the normal (absolute) vol of the short rate in decimal, and times are in years. The OIS forward rate is therefore higher than the futures rate. Equivalently, the futures price is higher (futures price = 100 − rate), which is why traders say futures "benefit from convexity" vs FRAs.

For a strip of SOFR futures, the adjustments compound: contract 1 (3M expiry) needs 0.003bp, contract 4 (1y expiry) needs 0.050bp, contract 20 (5y expiry) needs 1.3bp. Getting these wrong in a forward curve will misprice any product with long-dated SOFR exposure.

Glossary

Hover any term for a definition.

Convexity
The second derivative of bond price with respect to yield, divided by price; positive convexity means the bond gains more on rallies than it loses on selloffs of equal magnitude.
DV01
Dollar value of a 1bp move — the first-order rate sensitivity; for a $1M 10y bond at 4%, roughly $800.
Modified Duration
% price change per 1% (100bp) move in yield; equals DV01 × 10,000 / Price; a 10y bond at 4% has ~8.1y modified duration.
Dollar Convexity
½ × C × P × (Δy)² in dollar terms; the convexity P&L for a given move; grows with the square of the rate move.
Annuity (PV01)
Present value of 1bp per period across all swap cash flow dates; equals swap DV01 / notional / 0.0001; falls as rates rise.
CMS
Constant Maturity Swap — pays a fixed-tenor swap rate (e.g., 10y) at periodic dates; requires a convexity adjustment above the forward swap rate.
CMS Adjustment
The bp premium above the forward swap rate needed to fairly price a CMS receipt; typically 3–15bp depending on vol, payment time, and swap tenor.
Futures Convexity Adj
The bp discount to forward rates needed for SOFR/ED futures due to daily margining; = ½σ²T₁T₂, e.g., ~1.3bp for a 5y expiry at 100bp vol.
Jensen's Inequality
E[f(X)] ≥ f(E[X]) for convex f; the mathematical root of CMS and futures adjustments — expected swap rate under T-measure > forward swap rate.
Gamma (rates)
How much DV01 changes per bp move in rates; positive for long bonds/swaps; swaptions have explicit gamma, vanilla swaps have implicit gamma through annuity curvature.
Annuity Measure
The probability measure under which a swap's fixed-leg annuity is the numeraire; natural for swap pricing; swap rates are martingales under this measure.
Taylor Approximation
ΔP ≈ −Dur×P×Δy + ½×C×P×(Δy)²; the convexity term is the second-order correction; for moves >50bp the residual higher-order terms become non-negligible.
