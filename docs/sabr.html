The SABR Volatility Model

Understand how professional traders price options using a model that captures the shapes real markets produce — purely through interaction.

Why Black-Scholes isn't enough: Black-Scholes assumes every option on the same underlying has the same implied volatility — a flat surface. In reality, out-of-the-money puts trade at higher vol than ATM options (the "smile"), and puts are pricier than calls of equal distance (the "skew"). SABR models this directly by treating volatility itself as a random process.
Black-Scholes — Flat surface
SABR — Realistic surface

Think of implied volatility as the market's price tag for uncertainty. Under Black-Scholes the price tag is the same for every strike — imagine a perfectly flat tablecloth (left surface above).

SABR says: no, uncertainty itself is uncertain. Volatility can spike or collapse, and the direction of price moves is often correlated with those vol moves (negative correlation → crashes happen with vol spikes). This creates the curved, tilted surface on the right.

The four SABR parameters let you dial in the exact shape of that curvature, tilt, and level — matching what you actually see in listed options markets every day.

σ₀ — Initial Volatility 20%

The overall level of the vol surface — the "base price of uncertainty." Higher σ₀ means every option is more expensive. Calm markets: 10–15%. Stressed markets: 30%+.

α — Vol of Vol (curvature) 40%

How violently volatility itself jumps around. Higher α bows the wings of the smile upward — OTM options become relatively more expensive. Think post-crisis VIX spikes.

β — Elasticity (backbone) 0.50

How the ATM vol level moves when the forward price moves. Log-normal markets (β=1, equities) keep ATM vol roughly constant. Normal markets (β=0, rates) see ATM vol rise as the level falls.

ρ — Correlation (skew) −0.30

The correlation between price moves and vol moves. Negative ρ tilts the smile left — put vols rise above call vols (equity crash fear). Positive ρ tilts right (some commodity markets fear supply squeezes).

The SABR model specifies that the forward price F and its volatility α evolve as:

\[dF = \alpha\, F^\beta\, dW_1 \qquad d\alpha = \nu\,\alpha\, dW_2 \qquad \langle dW_1\,dW_2\rangle = \rho\,dt\]

Hagan et al. (2002) derived a fast closed-form approximation for the implied Black-Scholes vol this produces:

\[\sigma_B \approx \frac{\alpha}{(FK)^{\frac{1-\beta}{2}}} \cdot \frac{z}{\chi(z)} \cdot \left[1 + \left(\frac{(1-\beta)^2\alpha^2}{24(FK)^{1-\beta}} + \frac{\rho\beta\nu\alpha}{4(FK)^{\frac{1-\beta}{2}}} + \frac{(2-3\rho^2)\nu^2}{24}\right)T\right]\]

where \(z = \tfrac{\nu}{\alpha}(FK)^{\frac{1-\beta}{2}}\ln\tfrac{F}{K}\) and \(\chi(z)=\ln\!\left[\tfrac{\sqrt{1-2\rho z+z^2}+z-\rho}{1-\rho}\right]\). The ATM case (K≈F) uses a limiting form to avoid 0/0. This implied vol is then fed into the standard Black-Scholes formula to get the option price.

Volatility Smile Cross-Section

Slice the surface at a fixed maturity. The dashed grey line is what Black-Scholes assumes (flat at σ₀); the blue curve is the SABR smile. The gold dot marks ATM.

Forward = 100

ATM (K = 100): Where both curves meet. The SABR ATM vol equals σ₀ at T=0 and drifts slightly with the correction terms over time.

Left wing (K < 100 — downside strikes): Negative ρ pushes this higher. A trader paying more for a 90-strike put than BS suggests is buying crash insurance.

Right wing (K > 100 — upside strikes): Mostly shaped by α (vol of vol). High α lifts both wings roughly symmetrically before the ρ skew tilts the whole shape.

Maturity effect: Shorter maturities show sharper, more dramatic smiles. Longer maturities are flatter — vol uncertainty has more time to mean-revert.

Glossary

Hover any term for a definition.

Implied Volatility
The volatility number you reverse-engineer from a market option price using Black-Scholes. It's the market's consensus forecast of uncertainty, not a historical statistic.
Vol Surface
A 3-D map of implied vol across all strikes and maturities. In a perfect BS world it's flat; in reality it curves, tilts, and changes every trading day.
Vol Smile
A cross-section of the vol surface at one maturity. Named "smile" because implied vol is often higher at both OTM puts and calls, bowing upward from ATM.
Skew
When the smile is asymmetric — one side is higher than the other. Negative skew means OTM puts are pricier than equidistant OTM calls (equity crash fear).
ATM
At-The-Money: a strike equal to (or close to) the forward price. ATM options have maximum time value and greatest sensitivity to volatility changes.
Vol of Vol
How much the volatility level itself fluctuates. High vol-of-vol means vol can spike or collapse dramatically, producing fatter tails and more pronounced smile curvature.
Backbone
How the ATM vol level moves when the forward price moves. β=1 keeps ATM vol roughly constant as prices move; β=0 means ATM vol moves inversely with the price level.
Forward Price
The fair price for delivery of an asset at a future date. Options pricing uses the forward (not spot) to strip out carry costs and dividends.
Delta
How much the option price changes per $1 move in the forward. SABR and flat BS can give different deltas, especially for skewed smiles and OTM strikes.
CEV
Constant Elasticity of Variance — the backbone model embedded in SABR. The β parameter controls the CEV exponent, shaping how vol relates to the price level.
